That actually makes a lot of sense, and you’re not alone in feeling this way.

A few reasons why **learning** can feel way more draining than **coding**:

### 1. Coding is flow, learning is friction
When you code something you already understand, you’re in *execution mode*—pattern recognition, problem-solving, momentum.  
Learning forces you into *unknown territory*: you have to hold confusion in your head, constantly evaluate “do I get this?”, and fight the urge to fill gaps with guesses. That cognitive friction is exhausting.

### 2. Learning often lacks immediate feedback
With coding, you get fast loops:
> write → run → error → fix → success

With learning (books, courses, docs), feedback is slow or absent. Your brain hates ambiguity—it spends energy just *monitoring* whether progress is happening.

### 3. Passive input ≠ active use
A lot of “learning” is passive: reading, watching, listening. That feels stressful because you’re *receiving* without *doing*. Your brain is built to learn by **using**, not by absorbing.

### 4. Fear of wasted effort
When coding, effort = output.  
When learning, effort might = “I still don’t get it.” That uncertainty feels like risk, and risk is tiring.

---

### What helps (based on your style)
Since you thrive with coding:
- **Learn by building immediately** — don’t study a topic; use it in a tiny project
- **Timebox learning** (20–30 min), then switch to coding
- **Treat learning as reconnaissance**, not mastery — “I just need enough to try”

You’re not bad at learning—you’re just wired to learn *through doing*, not through intake.
